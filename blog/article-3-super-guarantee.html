SUPER GUARANTEE 2025–26: WHAT YOUR EMPLOYER OWES YOU

NewFinera · Updated June 2026 · 7 min read · General education only

WHAT IS THE SUPER GUARANTEE?

The Superannuation Guarantee (SG) is a compulsory employer contribution to your superannuation fund, legislated under the Superannuation Guarantee (Administration) Act 1992. It requires employers to contribute a percentage of your ordinary time earnings into a complying superannuation fund on your behalf.

Superannuation is Australia's mandatory retirement savings system. Money paid into your super fund is generally preserved until you reach your preservation age and satisfy a condition of release — it cannot simply be withdrawn like a bank account. Super contributions grow through investment returns within the fund, taxed at concessional rates.

THE 12% SG RATE

The SG rate has been increasing incrementally toward 12%. It reached 12% from 1 July 2025 and remains at 12% for 2025–26. Historical and legislated rates:

Financial YearSG Rate
2022–2310.5%
2023–2411.0%
2024–2511.5%
2025–2612.0%

The 12% rate applies to ordinary time earnings — your regular salary or wages. It does not apply to overtime, although some enterprise agreements and awards include overtime in the SG calculation base. Always check your employment contract and award.

WHO IS ELIGIBLE FOR THE SG?

Most employees are entitled to the Super Guarantee. Eligibility applies to:

  • Employees aged 18 or over, regardless of hours worked or earnings level (the previous $450/month threshold was removed from 1 July 2022)
  • Employees under 18 who work more than 30 hours per week
  • Casual employees, part-time employees, and full-time employees
  • Some contractors who are engaged primarily for labour

Certain categories may not be entitled to SG — including some non-resident employees, employees paid for work done overseas in certain circumstances, and some domestic or private workers working less than 30 hours per week. Consult the ATO or a professional for specific situations.

WHEN MUST EMPLOYERS PAY SG?

Employers must pay the Super Guarantee at least quarterly. The quarterly due dates are:

QuarterPayment Due Date
1 July – 30 September28 October
1 October – 31 December28 January
1 January – 31 March28 April
1 April – 30 June28 July

Important: SG contributions must be received by the super fund by the due date — not merely sent. If an employer misses the deadline or pays the wrong amount, they become liable for the Superannuation Guarantee Charge (SGC), which is not tax-deductible and includes interest and an administration fee. This is more expensive than simply paying on time.

HOW TO CHECK YOUR SUPER IS BEING PAID

Many Australians never verify their super is being paid correctly. Here are the general methods for checking, for educational purposes:

  • MyGov / ATO Online: Link your MyGov account to the ATO. Under "Super," you can see contributions reported by your employer. Note: employer contributions may appear on a slight delay
  • Super fund member portal: Log in to your super fund's online portal or app to see contributions received
  • Annual member statement: Your super fund sends an annual statement showing all contributions, investment returns, and fees
  • Payslip: Your payslip should show the SG amount being accrued or paid for the period

WHAT TO DO IF SUPER ISN'T BEING PAID

If you believe your employer is not paying your SG correctly, you can report this to the ATO using the "Report unpaid super" tool at ato.gov.au. The ATO can investigate and recover unpaid super on your behalf. This is general educational information — the ATO website provides specific guidance on the reporting process.

SALARY SACRIFICE — BOOSTING SUPER PRE-TAX

Salary sacrifice into superannuation is an arrangement where you agree to forgo a portion of your pre-tax salary in exchange for your employer making additional super contributions. Because these contributions are concessional (pre-tax), they are taxed at 15% inside the fund rather than at your marginal income tax rate.

The actual benefit depends on your marginal tax rate, total concessional contributions, Division 293 tax (which applies to high income earners), and other individual factors. Salary sacrifice arrangements require a written agreement with your employer and should be structured with professional advice.
